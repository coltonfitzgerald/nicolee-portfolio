Terminology

Iambic Pentameter

Iambic pentameter is a verse rhythm often used in Shakespeare’s writing. It's the name given to a line of verse that consists of five iambs. (an iamb being one unstressed syllable followed by one stressed, such as "before")
It has 10 syllables per line.

EXAMPLE:
"Two households, both alike in dignity,
In fair Verona, where we lay our scene,
From ancient grudge break to new mutiny,
Where civil blood makes civil hands unclean.
From forth the fatal loins of these two foes
A pair of star-cross'd lovers take their life;

Figurative Language Terms

Act -
An act is a part of a play defined by elements such as rising action, climax and resolution.
Example: Another word for "chapter" in a play. Everything that's labeled Act I - end of the story.
Allusion -
An expression designed to call something to mind without mentioning it explicitly; an indirect of passing phrase.
Example: "But all so soon as the all-cheering sun,
Should in the farthest East begin to draw,
The shady curtains from Aurora’s bed."
Anaphora -
A the use of a word referring to or replacing a word used earlier in a sentence, to avoid repetition.
Example: "This blessed plot, this earth, this realm, this England, This nurse, this teeming womb of royal kings...
This land of such dear souls, this dear land."
Aside -
Lines spoken by an actor that was intended for the audience and not other characters in the play.
Example: Oh, ‘tis too true!,
How smart a lash that speech doth give my conscience!
The harlot’s cheek, beautied with plastering art, Is not more ugly to the thing that helps it, Than is my ded to my post painted work.,
O heavy burden!"
Blank Verse -
A verse without rhyme, especially that which uses iambic pentameter.
Example: "Hark! What light in yonder window breaks?
It is the east, and Juliet is the sun."
Comic Relief -
Comic episodes in a dramatic or literary work that offset more serious sections.
Example: Rosemary and Romeo begin with [the same] "R" letter.
Drama -
A play for theater, radio, or television.
Epithet -
An adjective or descriptive phrase expressing a quality characteristic of the person or thing mentioned.
Example: "Death lies on her like an untimely frost."
Foil -
A character who contrasts with another character usually the protagonist in order to highlight particular qualities of the other character.
Example: Mercutio in "Romeo & Juliet"
Imagery -
Visually descriptive or figurative language, especially in a literary work.
Example: "tis an unweeded garden,; that grows to seed; things rank and gross in nature; possess it merely."
Irony - DRAMATIC -
The expression of one’s meaning by using language that normally signifies the opposite, typically for humorous or emphatic effect.
Irony - SITUATIONAL -
Occurs when the audience knows something the characters do not. Because of the understanding, the words of the characters take on a different meaning.
Irony - VERBAL -
Is most broadly defined as a situation where the outcome is incongruous with what was expected.
Metaphor -
A figure of speech in which a word or phrase is applied to an object or action to which it is not literally applicable.
Example: "Look, love, what envious streaks Do lace the severing clouds in yonder East:
Night’s candles are burnt out, and jocund day Stands tiptoe on the misty mountain tops."
Metonymy -
The substitution of the name of an attribute or adjunct for that of the thing meant.
Example: "Friends, Romans, countrymen, lend me your ears!"
Monologue -
A long speech by one actor in a play or movie, or as part of a theatrical or broadcast program.
Example: "O Romeo, Romeo, wherefore art thou Romeo?"
Oxymoron -
A figure of speech in which apparently contradictory terms appear in conjuction.
Example: "Parting is such sweet sorrow"
Periphrasis -
The use of indirect and circumlocutory speech or writing.
Example: "When that fell arrest Without all bail shall carry me away.."
Personification -
The attribution of a personal nature or human characteristics to something or the representation of an abstract quality in human form.
Example: "When well-appareled April on the heel Of limping winter treads."
Scene -
A scene is a part of an act defined with the changing characters.
Soliloquy -
An act of speaking one’s thoughts aloud when by oneself or regardless of any hearers especially by a character in a play.
Example: "To be, or not to be: that is the question"
Synecdoche -
A figure of speech in which a part is made to represent the while or vice versa.
Example: "O no! It is an ever-fixed mark That looks on tempests and is never shaken"
Wordplay -
The witty exploitation of the meanings and ambiguities of words.
Example: "Ask for me tomorrow, and you shall find me a grave man."
